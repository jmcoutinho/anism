The Woman Who Married a Cabinet

Frustrated with how material objects were being treated around her, Yvonne Dröge Wendel, an artist and at the time student at the Rietveld Academie, embarked on a quest to question and rethink how we relate to the material entities we interact with in our everyday lives, that still lasts today.

The project in focus here is the one Yvonne developed for her graduation in 1992. Taking her ideas to the extreme as a form of illustration, she committed to a lifelong marriage with a Wendel cabinet, with which she spent an enviable honeymoon in the south-east of Portugal.

See some photos of the ceremony in the artist's website, and read a full interview with Yvonne Dröge Wendel in issue nº5 of MacGuffin Magazine.

The History of Anism

Introduction

Anism is a new movement, but its ideals have been around for a long while. They were inherent to the beliefs and mythologies that guided the indigenous peoples that populated the lands we now inhabit. In fact, they were included in some of the patterns most commonly found by the Anthropologists who coined them under the term 'animism'.

Animism

Animism, in its own rights, is one of Anthropology's earliest concepts, as conceived by Sir Edward Tylor, the creator of its currently accepted definition.
It can be described as the belief that all material entities possess a 'distinct spiritual essence'. That the division between physical and spiritual is tenuous, all substantial matter requiring an equivalent degree of respect and consideration.

Anism

Anism, on its turn, is the urbanised cousin of animism. It's a movement that presupposes action for the change of behaviour and mentalities in a time in which disposability is the norm and materiality has lost the amplitude of its meaning in our lives.
The myths that founded our societies are outdated, the uncertainty they tried to enlighten is no longer of considerable relevance. We now, more than ever, need new foundational allegories that can help us confront the perils of unsustainability.
What anism proposes is that we take from the ideals of animism and apply them to our individualities and our time. That we look after and enlarge the auras – the value and integrity – of the material goods which comprise our lives: that we build on their histories, on their idiosyncrasies; that we know when to let go of them respectfully, always making sure the aura is leastwise maintained, never diminished and never lost.
